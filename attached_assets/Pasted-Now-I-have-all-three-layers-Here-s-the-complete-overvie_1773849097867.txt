The 8 Nutrition Phases
These are your dietary regime — the macronutrient strategy you're currently running. You select one in the qualitative log each day to record which protocol was active. They are not about physical performance directly; they describe how you're fueling and what the fuel distribution is doing.

Base
Your stabilized bulk baseline. Not pushing surplus hard, not cutting anything. This is the maintenance-adjacent operating point you return to between deliberate pushes — a controlled steady state where nothing is being forced in either direction.

Carb-Up
A deliberate increase in carbohydrate intake. The intent is to increase fuel pressure and raise training support capacity. Used when you want to drive harder training quality, fill glycogen more aggressively, or transition into a growth phase.

Carb-Cut
A reduction in carbohydrate intake specifically. The emphasis shifts toward careful fuel allocation — the body has less carbohydrate fuel to work with, which forces better nutrient routing. Often used to reduce body fat without touching dietary fat, or as a step down before a more aggressive cut.

Fat-Cut
A reduction in dietary fat specifically. The goal is reducing excess storage pressure and improving how nutrients are directed — less dietary fat means less fat available for storage and more pressure toward other substrates. Same routing logic as Carb-Cut but the dietary lever being pulled is different.

Recomp
Slow body recomposition — simultaneously trying to route nutrients toward muscle while keeping fat stable or declining. A genuinely difficult physiological state that requires precise intake. Used when you want to shift body composition without committing to a full bulk or cut cycle. Progress is slower than dedicated phases but the two goals are both being served.

Deload
Recovery nutrition. Caloric and training strain is reduced to allow CNS recovery, restore receptor sensitivity, and give the body time to consolidate adaptations. This is not a cut — it's intentional reduction of the stimulus to make the system more responsive to the next one.

Diet Break
A metabolic reset. When you've been in a sustained deficit and adaptation has started to suppress metabolic output (hunger hormones disrupted, performance declining, mood suppressed), a diet break brings intake back up to maintenance to relieve that adaptive suppression and restore output. Different from Deload in that the training doesn't necessarily change — the nutrition is what gets reset.

Peak Bulk
The most aggressive growth phase. Highest caloric surplus, highest carbohydrate pressure, strongest hypertrophy drive. You are pushing the system as hard as it will accept. Fatigue and fat gain risk are both highest here. Used when the goal is maximum muscle growth over a defined window before entering a correction phase.

The 7 Daily Meal Slots
These are the timing-based positions meals occupy within a day, independent of which nutrition phase you're running. Every nutrition phase has its own template for each slot — the combination (phase_slot, e.g. base_precardio) determines exactly what you eat.

Slot	Role
Pre-Cardio	Light activation fuel — minimal digestive drag, just enough to prime readiness and wake-up response
Post-Cardio	Recovery and substrate reset — begins restoring what cardio depleted, sets the metabolic tone for the morning
Mid-Morning	Stable anchor meal — steady, controlled, maintenance and routing oriented, no extremes
Pre-Lift	Explicit training fuel — carbohydrates and protein timed before major tension work
Post-Lift	Training recovery feed — absorb the training stimulus and begin supercompensation
Evening	Grounding and overnight support — nervous system settling, slower proteins, sets overnight restoration
Protein Reserve	Flexible buffer — used as needed to hit daily protein targets without being tied to a specific timing slot
The 8 Orbital Phases
This is the physiological interpretation layer — completely separate from what you're eating. The orbital phase describes what is happening inside the body at a cellular and systemic level: where it is in a growth, recovery, adaptation, or expression cycle. The nutrition-to-orbital translator table maps which nutrition phase most likely corresponds to which orbital state, but the orbital phase is a read — not something you directly select.

Think of orbital phases as answering: what is the body actually doing right now as a biological system?

1 — Priming
The system is being prepared and readied. Light activation, low digestive drag, no high-demand state yet. Corresponds to the early morning pre-training window. The body is receptive but not yet loaded.

2 — Loading
Force production is rising. Fuel input is increasing to support training tension. The system is being driven upward toward a peak output state. This is the build-up before the hardest work.

3 — Accumulation
The training stimulus is being absorbed. Substrates are being restored, muscle damage is beginning to be addressed, and the body is processing what the training session asked of it. Recovery is underway but is not complete.

4 — Saturation
Maximum cellular stress state. Carbohydrate pressure is highest, hypertrophy drive is at its peak. The system is fully loaded and under the most growth stimulus. This is the physiological state that corresponds to Peak Bulk — the body is being pushed to its capacity to adapt upward.

5 — Partitioning
The body is actively directing nutrients — routing carbohydrates and protein toward muscle rather than fat storage. This is an efficiency phase rather than a volume phase. Associated with Carb-Cut, Fat-Cut, and Recomp, where the emphasis is on where nutrients go rather than how many are coming in.

6 — Resensitization
Receptor sensitivity is being restored and the CNS is recovering. Overnight and deload periods fall here. After sustained loading, tissues and receptors become less responsive; resensitization is the body restoring its ability to respond strongly to the next stimulus.

7 — Rebound
Supercompensation expression — the body is relieving adaptive suppression and restoring output capacity. This is what happens after a deload or diet break when the suppressed systems bounce back. Strength often feels elevated in this phase, energy returns, and the body is more responsive than it was mid-deload.

8 — Expression
The system has stabilized at a higher baseline. Not pushing, not resetting — operating in a controlled, capable state. This corresponds to the Base phase: you've absorbed the previous cycle, you're not yet forcing the next one, and the body is expressing the adaptation that was built.

How the Two Systems Connect
The translator table is what connects nutrition phase → orbital interpretation. It's a lookup layer, not a merge of the concepts. Here's the full mapping:

Nutrition Phase	Primary Orbital	Secondary Orbital	Logic

Carb-Cut	Partitioning	Resensitization	Less fuel, more careful routing
Fat-Cut	Partitioning	Resensitization	Same routing logic, different dietary lever

The secondary orbital is what the state tends toward if the nutrition phase is sustained or if training intensity shifts — for example, Carb-Up sits in Loading but drifts toward Accumulation if you stay there long enough without a corresponding deload.
